<p class="recap">Recap from <a href="/lessons/0007-sitemaps-and-indexnow.html">Lesson 0007</a>: that got your page <em>found</em>. But found ≠ believed. Every prior lesson made the page eligible and discoverable; this one is about whether engines (and AI answer engines) have any reason to <em>trust</em> what it says.</p>

<p class="lede">E-E-A-T = <b>Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust</b> — the framework Google's quality raters use to judge content. Google is blunt about its centre: <em>"Of these aspects, trust is most important. The others contribute to trust."</em><sup>[1]</sup> And about the trap most people fall into next.</p>

## The myth, and the builder's opening

There is no "E-E-A-T score," no E-E-A-T meta tag, and it is <b>not a direct ranking factor</b>. Straight from Google:

> "While E-E-A-T itself isn't a specific ranking factor, using a mix of factors that can identify content with good E-E-A-T is useful."<sup>[1]</sup>

Read that as a spec. Google's systems approximate E-E-A-T with a <em>mix of factors</em> — and many of those factors are concrete, machine-readable things on your page. You can't compute the score, but you <b>can</b> audit the mix. That's the tool.

<Callout variant="win" lead="Your win:">run `eeat_audit.py` on a page and get a checklist of the detectable trust signals — byline, author's linked identity, publish/updated dates, publisher, outbound sourcing, transparency links — grouped by the E-E-A-T letter each supports. Explicitly <em>not</em> a score; a gap-finder.</Callout>

The higher the stakes, the higher the bar: Google holds <b>YMYL</b> pages — "Your Money or Your Life," anything affecting health, finances, safety, or societal well-being — to far stricter E-E-A-T.<sup>[2]</sup>

## From fuzzy guideline → detectable signal

This is the whole builder move: each abstract quality maps to something in your HTML or JSON-LD that a crawler can confirm in milliseconds.

| Quality | Machine-detectable proxy | Lives in |
|---------|--------------------------|----------|
| <span class="badge c">E·E</span> authorship | named author + author entity | `meta[author]`, `rel=author`, Article `author` |
| <span class="badge c">A</span> authority | author identity linked off-site | `Person.sameAs` → real profile |
| <span class="badge c">A</span> publisher | org behind the content | Article `publisher` |
| <span class="badge c">T</span> transparency | publish + updated dates | `datePublished`, `dateModified` |
| <span class="badge c">T</span> accountability | about / contact reachable | site nav / footer links |
| <span class="badge c">T</span> sourcing | cites outside references | outbound `<a>` to sources |

That last row is also an <span class="tag aeo">AEO</span> lever: the GEO research found that adding citations, quotations, and statistics measurably lifts how often AI engines cite a page.<sup>[3]</sup> Trust signals and AI-citation signals are the same signals.

## The tool: audit the mix

```python
# NOT a score. Each row is one factor in Google's "mix" — present or missing.
author_named  = meta[author] or rel=author or Article.author
author_linked = Person.sameAs -> an authoritative profile     # authority
trust = datePublished & dateModified & publisher
      & cites_outbound_sources & about/contact & https      # the centre
```

<Callout variant="gotcha" lead="You can't mark your way to authority.">These signals are <em>necessary, not sufficient</em>. A `sameAs` pointing at a profile that doesn't establish any real expertise fools no one — Authoritativeness is mostly earned <em>off</em> your page (who links to and cites the author). The audit finds the <em>missing</em> markup so engines can <em>read</em> the trust you've actually built; it can't manufacture trust you haven't. And on YMYL topics, faking it is actively risky.<sup>[2]</sup> Add the signals because they're true, then make them machine-readable.</Callout>

<p class="fineprint">Ceiling to know: `eeat_audit.py` checks <em>presence</em> of signals, not their quality — it confirms an author is named, not that the author is genuinely expert. It's a completeness gate on the parts a machine can verify, deliberately silent on the parts only a human rater can judge.</p>
