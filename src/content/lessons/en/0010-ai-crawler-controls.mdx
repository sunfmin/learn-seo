<p class="recap">Recap: <a href="/lessons/0002-crawlable-vs-indexable.html">Lesson 0002</a> used `robots.txt` to control <em>one</em> crawler — Googlebot. This lesson uses the same file to answer a question Googlebot never raised: now that a dozen AI crawlers fetch your pages, <b>which of them do you let in, and for what?</b></p>

<p class="lede">There is no PASS/FAIL here. Allowing or blocking an AI crawler is a <b>policy choice</b>, not a bug — so the goal is to <em>see your current posture</em> clearly and set it deliberately, instead of inheriting whatever default your CMS shipped.</p>

<Callout variant="win" lead="Your win:">read your site's exact ALLOW/BLOCK posture toward every major AI crawler — and understand the one distinction (train vs retrieve) that decides whether blocking helps you or quietly costs you citations.</Callout>

## A fetch can do three different jobs

The mistake is treating "AI bots" as one thing. A fetch falls into one of three buckets, and you may feel differently about each:

| Job | What the fetch feeds | Example bots |
|-----|----------------------|--------------|
| **train** | a model's training corpus | `GPTBot`, `Google-Extended`, `CCBot`, `ClaudeBot`, `Bytespider` |
| **retrieve** | a live answer it will *cite* | `OAI-SearchBot`, `PerplexityBot` |
| **user-fetch** | one page a user asked for | `ChatGPT-User`, `Claude-Web` |

<Callout variant="insight" lead="The decision that matters:">citations — the whole AEO win from <a href="/lessons/0001-the-two-pipelines.html">Lesson 0001</a> — come from the <b>retrieve</b> bots. Training comes from the <b>train</b> bots. They are <em>separate crawlers</em>, so you can refuse training and still be citable. Block the train bots, keep the retrieve bots open: opt out of the corpus without going invisible in answers.</Callout>

<Callout variant="gotcha" lead="The Google-Extended trap:">`Google-Extended` controls only whether your content trains <b>Gemini / Vertex AI</b>. It does <u>not</u> affect Google Search, and it does <u>not</u> control AI Overviews — those follow normal indexing.<sup>[1]</sup> Blocking `Google-Extended` to "keep AI out" does not remove you from Search, and does not stop AI Overviews from quoting your indexed pages. Different lever than people think.</Callout>

## robots.txt is a request, not a wall

Every directive here is **voluntary**. A well-behaved crawler reads `robots.txt` and obeys; a hostile scraper ignores it entirely.<sup>[2]</sup> If you must *enforce* a block, that's a server-side job — a WAF, rate limits, or UA/IP rules — not a line in a text file.

```text
# robots.txt — opt out of training, stay citable
User-agent: GPTBot
Disallow: /

User-agent: Google-Extended
Disallow: /

User-agent: OAI-SearchBot
Allow: /          # keep retrieval open → still citable
```

<Callout variant="note" lead="On llms.txt:">you'll see `llms.txt` pitched as "robots.txt for AI." It's a <b>proposal, not a standard</b> — no major engine enforces it, and Google has publicly called it unnecessary. Ship one if you like (it's harmless), but don't mistake it for a control that does anything today.</Callout>

## The skill: read your posture

`tools/ai_bots.py` reads your `robots.txt` the way each AI crawler would — `urllib.robotparser` answers `can_fetch(bot, "/")` per user-agent — and probes for `llms.txt`.

```python
# the heart of it — ask robots.txt, once per AI bot
rp = urllib.robotparser.RobotFileParser()
rp.parse(robots_text.splitlines())
for ua, vendor, kind in AI_BOTS:
    posture = "ALLOW" if rp.can_fetch(ua, "/") else "BLOCK"
```

3. Decide deliberately: are you **citable** (retrieve bots allowed) while controlling **training** (train bots set to taste)? Set it; don't inherit it.
</Callout>
