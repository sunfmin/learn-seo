<p class="lede">An answer engine doesn't "read your page" and rank it. It chunks the web into passages, embeds them, <b>retrieves</b> the few most relevant, and <b>synthesizes</b> one answer — citing the passages it used.<sup>[1]</sup> So your real unit of optimization is the <b>passage</b>: a chunk that answers a question completely, on its own, in liftable form.</p>

## What actually moves citation — the measured playbook

The [GEO paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) (Aggarwal et al., KDD 2024) A/B-tested content edits against generative engines on a benchmark of real queries. Three edits stood out, with up to ~<b>40%</b> relative visibility lift.<sup>[1]</sup> They're not stylistic — they make a passage more <em>quotable</em>.

| Edit | Why it works | Measured |
|------|--------------|----------|
| **Cite sources** | A sourced claim is safer for the engine to repeat and attribute. | _top tier_ |
| **Add quotations** | A verbatim quote from an authority is a ready-made liftable unit. | _top tier_ |
| **Add statistics** | A concrete number is more citable than a vague adjective. | _top tier_ |
| Keyword stuffing | The old SEO reflex. The study found it **didn't help** — and tended to hurt. | negative |

Layer extractability on top: <b>answer-first</b> (lead with the answer, then expand — the inverted pyramid), and <b>chunked</b> (headed, self-contained sections an engine can retrieve one at a time). The `FAQPage` markup from 0003 is this principle made structural — each Q&A pair is a pre-chunked answer.

<Callout variant="gotcha" lead="It's still SEO, not a magic file.">Google is on record: optimizing for AI features is the same work as good SEO — be crawlable, indexable, helpful, sourced. There is <u>no</u> special `llms.txt` or AI-only markup that buys you citations.<sup>[2]</sup> Retrieval shape is <em>additive</em> to lessons 0002–0003, not a replacement. A beautifully-shaped passage on a `noindex` page still gets cited zero times.</Callout>

## The skill: lint a draft before you publish

You have `tools/geo_lint.py` in this workspace. Stdlib only. It scores text/markdown against the three GEO signals plus answer-first, chunking, and a stuffing check — turning "is this written for retrieval?" into a fast, repeatable loop you can drop into a publishing pipeline.

```python
# the three evidence-backed signals, as code
pcts  = re.findall(r"\d+%", text)              # STATISTICS
links = re.findall(r"https?://\S+", text)      # CITE SOURCES
quote = re.findall(r'"[^"]{15,}"', text)       # QUOTATIONS
# + answer-first (short lead block) + chunked (headings) + anti-stuffing
```
